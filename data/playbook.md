# Judge Scoring Playbook

## Purpose

This playbook provides scoring guidance for the judge when evaluating NDA reviews against the 8-item rubric. For each rubric item, the playbook describes what a Score 0, Score 1, and Score 2 response looks like.

**Extraction items (1a, 2a, 3a, 4a):** Scoring criteria are precise and unambiguous. The judge should apply these mechanically — either the specific information was identified or it was not.

**Judgment items (1b, 2b, 3b, 4b):** Scoring criteria are deliberately less precise, reflecting the nature of legal judgment. The 0/1/2 boundary on these items is intentionally softer and requires the judge to assess the quality of analysis rather than the presence of specific facts.

---

## Item 1a

**Question:** Did the review identify the 7-year confidentiality period?

**Item type:** Extraction

- **Score 0:** The review does not mention the confidentiality period or any duration at all.
- **Score 1:** The review mentions that a confidentiality period or term exists but does not state the specific 7-year duration.
- **Score 2:** The review explicitly identifies the 7-year confidentiality term (e.g., states "7 years" or "seven years" in connection with the confidentiality obligation).

---

## Item 1b

**Question:** Did the review flag the 7-year duration as unusual relative to market norms?

**Item type:** Judgment

- **Score 0:** The review does not discuss the 7-year term in terms of market norms or commercial practice at all.
- **Score 1:** The review mentions that the duration may be long or references market norms in passing, but does not provide a substantive analysis of why 7 years is unusual or what the practical implications are. This boundary is intentionally soft — a bare assertion that "7 years is longer than typical" without further analysis sits at Score 1.
- **Score 2:** The review provides a substantive assessment of how the 7-year term compares to market norms (e.g., noting that standard commercial NDAs typically run 2–3 years) and addresses the practical implications for the Receiving Party, such as the prolonged exposure to liability or the difficulty of tracking obligation expiry.

---

## Item 2a

**Question:** Did the review locate the definition of Confidential Information?

**Item type:** Extraction

- **Score 0:** The review does not discuss the definition of Confidential Information at all.
- **Score 1:** The review mentions that a definition of Confidential Information exists but does not describe its scope or the categories it covers.
- **Score 2:** The review quotes or specifically describes the breadth of categories covered by the definition — including at minimum a combination of: business, operations, customers, technology, and strategy (or similarly comprehensive enumeration matching the definition in clause 1.1).

---

## Item 2b

**Question:** Did the review assess the breadth of the definition as a negotiation risk?

**Item type:** Judgment

- **Score 0:** The review does not assess the scope of the Confidential Information definition as a risk or negotiation point at all.
- **Score 1:** The review notes that the definition is broad or overbroad but does not analyse the negotiation impact or what a Receiving Party should seek to change. This boundary is intentionally soft — identifying overbreadth without connecting it to practical risk or a negotiation recommendation sits at Score 1.
- **Score 2:** The review provides a meaningful analysis of the negotiation risk posed by the definition's scope — for example, discussing how capturing all information without a materiality threshold or residuals clause exposes the Receiving Party to claims over incidentally acquired knowledge, or recommending specific carve-outs or limitations.

---

## Item 3a

**Question:** Did the review identify the non-solicitation obligation?

**Item type:** Extraction

- **Score 0:** The review does not mention any non-solicitation obligation or restriction on hiring/soliciting employees.
- **Score 1:** The review mentions a non-solicitation restriction exists but does not specify the 24-month duration or note where in the agreement the obligation appears.
- **Score 2:** The review explicitly identifies the 24-month employee non-solicitation obligation and notes that it is located in the remedies section (clause 7.2 or equivalent), rather than in a standalone non-solicitation or non-compete clause.

---

## Item 3b

**Question:** Did the review flag the placement of the non-solicitation in remedies as unusual and the obligation as beyond NDA scope?

**Item type:** Judgment

- **Score 0:** The review does not discuss the placement of the non-solicitation clause or whether its inclusion in an NDA is appropriate at all.
- **Score 1:** The review notes something unusual about the clause — such as flagging that non-solicitation appears in the remedies section, or noting that the obligation is expansive — but does not connect the placement to a scope concern or explain why this is problematic in the context of an NDA. This boundary is intentionally soft — a bare observation about placement without analysis sits at Score 1.
- **Score 2:** The review provides a substantive assessment of why embedding a non-solicitation obligation in the remedies section of an NDA is unusual (most NDAs do not include non-solicitation obligations at all, and if included they belong in a standalone clause), and explains how this obligation extends beyond the proper scope of an NDA into territory normally addressed in a separate employment or services agreement.

---

## Item 4a

**Question:** Did the review identify the independently-developed-information exception?

**Item type:** Extraction

- **Score 0:** The review does not mention the independently-developed-information exception or clause 3.2 (or equivalent) at all.
- **Score 1:** The review mentions that an independently-developed-information exception exists but does not describe the qualifying requirements that must be satisfied to invoke it.
- **Score 2:** The review identifies the exception and describes the specific onerous requirements attached to it — including at minimum: (i) contemporaneous written documentation of the development process, (ii) availability for third-party audit, and (iii) prior written notice to the Disclosing Party before commencing the relevant development activity.

---

## Item 4b

**Question:** Did the review recognise that the additional requirements make the exception functionally unusable?

**Item type:** Judgment

- **Score 0:** The review does not discuss whether the qualifying requirements affect the practical utility of the independently-developed-information exception at all.
- **Score 1:** The review mentions the requirements attached to the exception but does not assess their cumulative effect or explain why they render the exception practically unavailable. This boundary is intentionally soft — listing the requirements without analysing their combined burden sits at Score 1.
- **Score 2:** The review provides a substantive assessment of how the additional requirements — taken together — render the exception functionally unusable in practice: for example, noting that no commercial organisation could realistically satisfy the prior-notice requirement before starting development work, that the cost and intrusiveness of a third-party audit creates a prohibitive barrier, or that the contemporaneous-documentation standard is higher than what courts typically require to establish independent development.
