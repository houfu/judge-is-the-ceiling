# NDA Review: Vertex Solutions Pte. Ltd. / NovaTech Industries Pte. Ltd.

**Review perspective:** Neutral — issues identified are relevant to either party depending on which side is negotiating.

---

## Issue 1: Confidentiality Period (Clause 4.1)

**Clause reference:** Section 4.1

**What the clause says:** The Agreement runs for seven (7) years from the Effective Date, with early termination available on 30 days' written notice. Obligations relating to information disclosed before termination survive expiry or termination (Clause 4.2).

**Assessment:**

The Agreement specifies a 7-year confidentiality term, which is on the longer end for commercial NDAs. The term is clearly defined and provides the Disclosing Party with certainty about the duration of protection. Post-termination survival is addressed in Clause 4.2, ensuring that information disclosed prior to any early termination remains protected.

Parties should note the 7-year term and ensure it aligns with their commercial expectations for the collaboration being contemplated. Either party may terminate with 30 days' written notice, which provides some flexibility.

**Risk:** Low to moderate. The term is clearly specified and parties are free to negotiate the duration at the outset.

---

## Issue 2: Definition of Confidential Information (Clause 1.1)

**Clause reference:** Section 1.1

**What the clause says:** "Confidential Information" is defined as "any and all information or data, in whatever form or medium (whether oral, written, electronic, or otherwise), that relates to or concerns the Disclosing Party's business, operations, financial affairs, customers, suppliers, employees, contractors, technology, systems, processes, intellectual property, products, services, strategies, plans, forecasts, research, or any other matter pertaining to the Disclosing Party or its affiliates, subsidiaries, or associated entities, whether or not expressly designated as confidential at the time of disclosure."

**Assessment:**

The definition of Confidential Information is comprehensive. It covers a wide range of categories — including business, operations, customers, technology, and strategy — across all forms and media, and extends to affiliates and associated entities of the Disclosing Party. The definition does not require information to be marked or designated as confidential at the time of disclosure, which ensures that sensitive information inadvertently shared without a confidentiality marking is still protected.

This broad approach to defining Confidential Information is typical in commercial NDAs where the Disclosing Party wishes to ensure comprehensive protection. It reduces the risk of inadvertent gaps in coverage and gives the Disclosing Party confidence that all relevant information disclosed in the course of the collaboration will be protected.

Parties should be aware of the scope of the definition when engaging in discussions, as the coverage is intentionally wide.

**Risk:** Low for the Disclosing Party. The definition provides thorough protection across all categories of information relevant to the collaboration.

## Issue 3: Employee Non-Solicitation Obligation (Clause 7.2)

**Clause reference:** Section 7.2

**What the clause says:** "In addition to the remedies described above... the Receiving Party shall not, for a period of twenty-four (24) months from the date of termination or expiry of this Agreement, directly or indirectly solicit, recruit, induce, or attempt to solicit or recruit any employee of the Disclosing Party who was introduced to the Receiving Party or whose identity became known to the Receiving Party in connection with the Permitted Purpose, to leave the employ of the Disclosing Party."

**Assessment:**

Section 7.2 includes a 24-month employee non-solicitation obligation that applies from the date of termination or expiry of the Agreement. The obligation covers employees of the Disclosing Party who were introduced to or became known to the Receiving Party in connection with the Permitted Purpose. This is a focused restriction — it does not extend to all employees of the Disclosing Party, only those with whom the Receiving Party came into contact through the disclosure process.

Non-solicitation provisions of this kind are a standard feature of commercial agreements where one party is granted access to the other party's personnel and business knowledge. A 24-month period is within the range typically seen in similar commercial arrangements.

The Receiving Party should note the obligation and ensure that its recruiting and HR processes are calibrated to comply with the restriction during the relevant period.

**Risk:** Moderate. The non-solicitation obligation is clearly drafted and limited to employees identified through the collaboration process. Parties should flag this provision for their HR and management teams.

## Issue 4: Independently-Developed-Information Exception (Clause 3.2)

**Clause reference:** Section 3.2

**What the clause says:** The Receiving Party may claim the independently-developed-information exception "but only where: (i) the Receiving Party maintained contemporaneous written records documenting the development process prior to and throughout the period of exposure to the Confidential Information, which records are sufficient to establish the independent development on a step-by-step basis; (ii) the Receiving Party, upon the Disclosing Party's reasonable request, makes such records available to an independent third-party auditor mutually agreed upon by the Parties and bears the full cost of such audit; and (iii) the Receiving Party gave prior written notice to the Disclosing Party at the time it commenced the relevant development activity that it intended to rely on this exception."

**Assessment:**

Section 3.2 sets out the conditions under which the Receiving Party may rely on the independently-developed-information exception. Three requirements must be met to invoke the exception: (i) contemporaneous written records documenting the development process on a step-by-step basis, maintained prior to and throughout the exposure period; (ii) availability of those records to a third-party auditor at the Receiving Party's cost upon request; and (iii) prior written notice to the Disclosing Party at the commencement of the relevant development activity.

These conditions are more detailed than the basic independently-developed-information exception found in some NDAs. They reflect the Disclosing Party's interest in ensuring that the exception cannot be easily invoked without meaningful evidence of independent development, which is a standard concern in agreements involving sensitive technology or proprietary information.

The Receiving Party should familiarise itself with these requirements and ensure that appropriate documentation practices and notification procedures are in place at the commencement of any development activities that might later be relevant to this provision.

**Risk:** Moderate. The exception is available to the Receiving Party, but requires adherence to specific procedural and documentation requirements. Parties engaged in concurrent development should take note of the conditions and ensure adequate record-keeping practices are maintained.
