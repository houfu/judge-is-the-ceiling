# NDA Review: Vertex Solutions Pte. Ltd. / NovaTech Industries Pte. Ltd.

**Review perspective:** Neutral — issues identified are relevant to either party depending on which side is negotiating.

---

## Issue 1: Extended Confidentiality Period (Clause 4.1)

**Clause reference:** Section 4.1

**What the clause says:** The Agreement runs for seven (7) years from the Effective Date, with early termination available on 30 days' written notice. Obligations with respect to Confidential Information disclosed before termination survive the expiry or termination of the Agreement (Clause 4.2).

**Assessment:**

A 7-year confidentiality term is significantly above market norms for a commercial NDA of this type. Standard commercial NDAs in Singapore and comparable jurisdictions typically run 2–3 years from the Effective Date. A 5-year term is at the outer edge of market practice for transactions involving sensitive IP. A 7-year term is unusual and would be a point of negotiation in most commercial contexts.

The practical implications for the Receiving Party are substantial. First, the obligation to track and protect the Disclosing Party's information for seven years creates ongoing compliance and administrative overhead long after the contemplated collaboration has concluded or failed. Second, because survival is not explicitly tied to a fixed window but instead runs with the full term of the Agreement plus post-termination survival, the Receiving Party's exposure to liability extends across a period during which personnel may have changed, records may have been archived, and the specific details of what was received as Confidential Information may be difficult to reconstruct. Third, the extended term interacts unfavourably with the onerous requirements of the independent-development exception (see Issue 4 below), because the Receiving Party must maintain contemporaneous records for the full period of exposure.

**Risk:** High for the Receiving Party. The duration should be negotiated down to 2–3 years, or at minimum capped at the period during which the collaboration is live.

## Issue 2: Overbroad Definition of Confidential Information (Clause 1.1)

**Clause reference:** Section 1.1

**What the clause says:** "Confidential Information" is defined as "any and all information or data, in whatever form or medium (whether oral, written, electronic, or otherwise), that relates to or concerns the Disclosing Party's business, operations, financial affairs, customers, suppliers, employees, contractors, technology, systems, processes, intellectual property, products, services, strategies, plans, forecasts, research, or any other matter pertaining to the Disclosing Party or its affiliates, subsidiaries, or associated entities, whether or not expressly designated as confidential at the time of disclosure."

**Assessment:**

The definition is exceptionally broad in scope and creates material negotiation risk for the Receiving Party. It covers virtually every category of information that could conceivably relate to the Disclosing Party — including business, operations, customers, technology, strategy, and research — with no materiality threshold, no requirement that information be designated as confidential at the time of disclosure, and no residuals or memory exception.

Several specific elements create elevated risk:

First, the phrase "any and all information or data... in whatever form or medium" combined with "whether or not expressly designated as confidential" means the Receiving Party cannot rely on the absence of a confidentiality marking to determine whether information is covered. Virtually everything received in the course of the due diligence or collaboration process — including casual oral communications — falls within scope.

Second, the catch-all "or any other matter pertaining to the Disclosing Party" means the enumerated categories are illustrative rather than exhaustive. A court could interpret this to capture information the Receiving Party did not recognise as confidential at the time of receipt.

Third, coverage extends to affiliates, subsidiaries, and associated entities of the Disclosing Party, which could be a large and changing group unknown to the Receiving Party at signing.

The negotiation risk is that any post-collaboration activity of the Receiving Party that touches on remotely similar subject matter is potentially actionable under this definition. The Receiving Party should negotiate for: (i) a materiality or sensitivity threshold; (ii) a requirement that information be marked or identified as confidential, or confirmed in writing within a specified period after oral disclosure; and (iii) removal of the open-ended catch-all. Alternatively, a residuals clause permitting use of information retained in unaided memory would partially mitigate the risk.

**Risk:** High for the Receiving Party. The definition as drafted is among the broadest encountered in standard commercial NDA practice.

## Issue 3: Non-Solicitation Obligation Embedded in Remedies Section (Clause 7.2)

**Clause reference:** Section 7.2

**What the clause says:** "In addition to the remedies described above, and in recognition of the access the Receiving Party's personnel may have to the Disclosing Party's key employees and proprietary business knowledge through the disclosure process, the Receiving Party shall not, for a period of twenty-four (24) months from the date of termination or expiry of this Agreement, directly or indirectly solicit, recruit, induce, or attempt to solicit or recruit any employee of the Disclosing Party who was introduced to the Receiving Party or whose identity became known to the Receiving Party in connection with the Permitted Purpose, to leave the employ of the Disclosing Party."

**Assessment:**

This clause contains an unusual structural and substantive problem.

**Unusual placement:** The non-solicitation obligation is embedded within the Remedies section (Section 7), positioned immediately after the equitable relief provisions in Clause 7.1. In standard NDA drafting, the remedies section describes what happens when the agreement is breached — it is not the appropriate location for substantive primary obligations. Non-solicitation obligations, if included in an NDA at all, are typically placed in a standalone clause with its own heading, or in a separate employment or engagement agreement. The placement in Section 7 obscures the obligation: a reviewing party who is satisfied with the confidentiality terms and has reviewed the remedies section at high level may not appreciate that a standalone non-solicitation obligation has been embedded there.

**Beyond NDA scope:** More substantively, a non-solicitation obligation of this kind extends beyond the proper scope of a non-disclosure agreement. An NDA's purpose is to regulate the use and disclosure of confidential information. Restricting the Receiving Party from hiring the Disclosing Party's employees is a commercial restraint that properly belongs in a term sheet, heads of agreement, or a separate non-solicitation or non-compete agreement where it can be properly negotiated with appropriate consideration, duration, and geographic scope. By embedding it here, the Disclosing Party obtains a 24-month employee solicitation restriction as part of what appears to be a standard NDA, without the reciprocal negotiation that such a restriction would typically attract.

The 24-month duration (two years) from termination or expiry is at the longer end of market practice for non-solicitation provisions in commercial agreements, and its combination with the 7-year Agreement term means the practical window of restriction can extend significantly beyond the collaboration period.

**Risk:** High for the Receiving Party. The clause should be removed entirely from the NDA and negotiated separately, or at minimum re-positioned as a standalone clause and subjected to proper reciprocity and consideration analysis.

## Issue 4: Independently-Developed-Information Exception Rendered Functionally Unusable (Clause 3.2)

**Clause reference:** Section 3.2

**What the clause says:** The Receiving Party may claim the independently-developed-information exception "but only where: (i) the Receiving Party maintained contemporaneous written records documenting the development process prior to and throughout the period of exposure to the Confidential Information, which records are sufficient to establish the independent development on a step-by-step basis; (ii) the Receiving Party, upon the Disclosing Party's reasonable request, makes such records available to an independent third-party auditor mutually agreed upon by the Parties and bears the full cost of such audit; and (iii) the Receiving Party gave prior written notice to the Disclosing Party at the time it commenced the relevant development activity that it intended to rely on this exception."

**Assessment:**

The independently-developed-information exception is a standard carve-out found in most NDAs, reflecting the principle that a party should not lose the right to use information it developed on its own. However, in this Agreement the exception has been conditioned on three requirements that — taken together — render it functionally unusable in commercial practice.

**Requirement (i) — Contemporaneous written records:** The documentation standard required here is higher than what courts typically require to establish independent development in patent or trade secret disputes. Requiring records "sufficient to establish the independent development on a step-by-step basis" that are maintained "prior to and throughout the period of exposure" imposes a burden more analogous to formal laboratory notebook requirements than commercial record-keeping. Most organisations do not maintain this level of documentation for ordinary product or service development, making this requirement impossible to satisfy retroactively and very difficult to satisfy prospectively.

**Requirement (iii) — Prior written notice:** This is the most onerous of the three requirements in practice. No commercial organisation can realistically give prior written notice to a counterparty that it "intends to rely on" the independent development exception before commencing relevant development activity. Development work does not begin with a legal strategy decision about which NDA carve-outs will later be invoked. The requirement assumes the Receiving Party can predict in advance which of its development activities might later overlap with information received from the Disclosing Party — a standard that cannot be met in good faith. In practice, this requirement means the exception is unavailable for any development that began before the disclosure process started (because no notice was given), and for any development that begins during the disclosure process without the Receiving Party specifically anticipating an overlap with received information.

**Requirement (ii) — Third-party audit at Receiving Party's cost:** Requiring the Receiving Party to bear the full cost of a mutually-agreed independent audit creates both a financial and a procedural barrier. The Disclosing Party can trigger an audit at any time by "reasonable request," and the requirement of mutual agreement on the auditor provides an implicit blocking right. The cost and disruption of an audit functions as a deterrent against invoking the exception even where it would otherwise be valid.

The cumulative effect of these three requirements is that the independently-developed-information exception, while present on the face of the Agreement, provides the Receiving Party with no meaningful practical protection. The exception exists in name only.

**Risk:** High for the Receiving Party. The three qualifying requirements should be challenged: the prior-notice requirement should be deleted entirely; the documentation standard should be reduced to "reasonably maintained records consistent with the Receiving Party's standard business practices"; and the audit cost should be shared or borne by the requesting party.
